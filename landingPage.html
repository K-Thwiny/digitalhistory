Slavery in Togoland
Different institutions of slavery operated side-by-side in the Volta Region: chattel and community-based slavery. We will learn about how German missionaries relied on both forms of unfree labor to expand their congregation.
German Missionaries
The North German Mission purchased over a hundred children and converted many of them to Christianity. In this section, we will learn about their religious worldview, views on slavery, and the mission's history.
Life at the Mission
Children, both purchased and free, found both acceptance and alienation at the mission stations. In this section, we will explore religious practices, expectations, and resistance at the mission stations.
German Criticism of Slavery
While many missionaries practiced slaveholding, a select group of Germans highlighted the growth of slavery in Togoland: GA Krause. We will learn how the German government censored speach and exiled Krause.
African Resistance to German Slaveholding
The urban elites in Lome organize against German policies that condone conditions that result in slavery. We will examine the city's layout to understand how the protests unfolded.
Lesson Plans
Learn how to incorporate the material on this website to create engaging lesson plans that explore slavery, religion, and colonialism in Africa.
About This Website

Welcome to my digital history project on slavery, christianity, and colonialism. I have reimagined my dissertation research in concrete modules that allow visitors to explore material, listen to archival documents, examine historical maps, among other activities. Each module examine a key theme of my research. I have also created lesson plans for secondary school educators. These lesson plans will help explore differing conceptions of slavery, the concept of agency and its limitations, and political consciousness under colonial rule.

The Volta Region and what became German Togoland was awash in slavery and only intensified when the German government arrived in 1884. While Britain and France, no paragons of abolition in Africa, Germany more explicitly attempted foster an environment where slaves could be brought into the colony. This increase in slaveholding has its roots when the North German Mission arrived in the 1840s. After an initial failure to establish a congregation, missionaries turned towards purchasing enslaved children and prosletyzing them. Click on "Slavery in Togoland" to find out more
